### Q5: New business opportunities - which ones should we present?

This is the empowerment part. Here are potential "new agency models" to discuss. Pick all that feel relevant (multiple choice):

- [A] **Outcome-based pricing** - Stop selling hours, start selling results. "We'll build your MVP for 50K fixed, delivered in 4 weeks." Higher margins because agents do the heavy lifting. **[RECOMMENDED]**
- [B] **AI-augmented products/services** - Build products that have agents baked in. Don't just build software, build software that runs itself. Offer "living software" that includes agent-based maintenance.
- [C] **Speed as differentiator** - "We deliver in 2 weeks what others deliver in 3 months." Win deals on speed, not on price. The agency becomes a rapid deployment force.
- [D] **Strategic consulting + execution** - Move up the value chain. Clients need someone who understands their business AND can execute with agents. The agency becomes a "transformation partner."
- [E] **Agent development as a service** - Build custom agents for clients. Not websites, not apps - agents that automate the client's own processes.
- [F] **Managed agent operations** - Like managed hosting, but for agents. Run and maintain agent fleets for clients. Recurring revenue.

I find outcome-based pricing to be tricky we call that actually just a fixed-price project. If an agency really wants to participate in the financial outcome, they also have to take the risks and the time delay. The outcome might show only after one or two years or might not show at all. How should the agency pay the salaries then? That's actually just selling the old services with a different semantic on the pricing. I don't think that's the way to go.

Speed as a differentiator is for sure something that will work short-term because most agencies did not do the switch yet to agentic engineering.

I think the real power is in focusing on supporting the companies with introducing AI and AI agents to their own offerings and also internal organization. For instance agency could specialize in replacing human labor by AI agents. There is an obvious financial benefit so the costs of this transition are not super relevant. Building an agent organization on scale is actually a very hard technical challenge. It is a distributed system with lots of non-deterministic behavior, quite an engineering challenge. Plus there are many organizational challenges that can be addressed.

Or you might also question the existing portfolio of the customer company, which most of the agency's customers have actually. They have the same problem. Their business model might be under attack. I see huge demand for answers on these challenges and this is always a mix of business and technical perspectives. You need to really be an expert in AI agents and energetic engineering to see the second and third-order effects.

I think agencies cannot expect that customers know what they want. Most humans do not know what AI and especially AI agents can offer to them so it's the agency's job to envison tailored solutions. Let's have in mind that almost every company on earth has to change its own organization and its offering in a super short period of time or competition will eat them for breakfast.

### Q6: Code quality in the Agentic Age - what's your take?

Jan specifically asked about this. How do you want to frame it?

- [A] **"Code quality matters MORE, not less"** - Agents produce massive amounts of code. Without discipline (testing, architecture, reviews), you drown in unmaintainable garbage. The senior engineer's role shifts from writing code to governing code. **[RECOMMENDED]**
- [B] **"Code quality is different now"** - Traditional metrics (clean code, patterns) matter less. What matters: test coverage, clear interfaces, deployability. If an agent can refactor it in 5 minutes, readability is less critical.
- [C] **"It depends on the layer"** - Core architecture and interfaces: quality matters more than ever. Implementation details: agents can churn through those, so quality is less critical.
- [D] **"Code is becoming disposable"** - If agents can rewrite modules faster than you can refactor them, maybe the answer is: write fast, test well, rewrite often.

When doing agentic engineering the code becomes a reputation detail. What's important now is knowing what to build: what increases revenue, decreases costs. Then translating this into requirements that are a mixture of functional requirements and technical specifications. And also important is to think how a new feature or a new product has what kind of business effect. Thinking through in advance what to expect from a feature, making sure the success is measurable, and then actually measuring it and showing it to the customer. That's a best practice we always wanted to do but now it's super easy and doable. The actual coding is a no-brainer; coding ages will do it. The code quality must still be high but developers care less and less about the actual code. Coding age makes sure it's working, has no bugs, and it's up to date, secure, etc. It requires some practice for sure but it's possible given the current state of coding agents.

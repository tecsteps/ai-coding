# Participant Round

### **What**

The idea is to let people introduce themselves to each other so everyone knows who is in the room

- **Mini-survey reveal on stage. (5min)** A fast read on who is here, which size brackets, where the room sits on agentic maturity, and which challenges come up the most (this is the end of the keynote)
- **Table intros and discussion. (30min)** At each table, everyone shares who they are, where they are with agentic engineering, and the biggest open question they are wrestling with right now. Then the table has a short discussion and agrees on two things: one shared headline that captures their common reality, and one hardest open question that nobody at the table has a confident answer to.
- **Nominee readout on the mic. (10min)** One nominee per table reads out the shared headline and the open question. Those open questions go on a wall board that stays up for the rest of the day, so the rest of the bootcamp has something concrete to chase.

# Host briefing

### Questions

- **Who are you?** Name, company, team size, your role.
- **Where are you with introducing Agentic Engineering to your org?**
- **What is your biggest open question this week?**

### **Table discussion (~6 minutes)**

Once everyone has spoken, the table agrees on two things:

1. **Headline.** One sentence that captures the shared reality at this table. Not a platitude. Something that would make another table nod or push back.
2. **Hardest open question.** The single question nobody at this table has a confident answer to. This goes to the wall board for the rest of the day.

### **Nominee (last minute)**

Pick one person at the table to read the headline and the open question on the mic. ~40 seconds, no elaboration.

# Drafted topics

⚠️ These topics are not fixed. If something doesn’t fit your participants, adjust or replace it. What matters most is an engaging discussion, not sticking to predefined questions.

Ideally you ask participants about the topics they want to discuss!

### (1) Rollout and resistance

Engineers are not adopting agentic engineering on their own. Training a handful of enthusiasts is easy. Getting the entire IT org, or the entire company, on it is not. What does that rollout look like in practice?

**Two pointed questions:**

1. Who is your most resistant engineer or manager, and what is their real objection under the stated one?
2. What part of the rollout have you tried and failed at, or not started because you do not know how?

**Questions if the table stalls:**

- Did licenses alone move anything?
- What did the click moment look like for one skeptic?
- Enthusiasts-first, manager-first, or all-hands rollout?
- What does rollout beyond engineering even mean in your company?

### **(2) Governance, compliance and procurement**

This is the topic everyone is dealing with privately and few are willing to describe publicly. Tool approval, legal review, data residency, auditability, liability, purchasing model, and token cost blow-ups are not edge cases. They are becoming the actual rollout gate.

**Two pointed questions:**

1. What is the single biggest approval, compliance, or procurement blocker you are stuck on right now?
2. What did legal, security, or procurement say no to that you still do not have a clean answer for?

**Prompts if the table stalls:**

- Formal procurement or hidden inside an existing tooling budget?
- Where does your code or data actually go?
- Who signs off on production or customer data exposure?
- What pricing model breaks first at scale?

### **(3) Proof of value, metrics and budget**

Everyone says developers are faster. Almost nobody has a measurement model they fully trust. Meanwhile the CFO, CEO, or board will ask for proof, and "people feel more productive" is not a durable answer.

**Two pointed questions:**

1. What number are you currently using to justify agent adoption that you do not fully trust yourself?
2. What should you probably be measuring, but have not started because it is hard, political, or ambiguous?

**Prompts if the table stalls:**

- Which speed metric actually matters?
- Which quality metric must sit next to it?
- Individual uplift versus average-team uplift: which is the real number?
- If output rises and quality drops, what is your stop signal?

### **(4) Org design, talent and management for 2027**

Once the room has talked through rollout, control, and proof, the strategic question becomes unavoidable: if implementation gets cheaper, what becomes scarce, and how should the IT organization change?

**Two pointed questions:**

1. Which role, layer, or team boundary in your current org is already under pressure from agentic engineering?
2. If you hire fewer juniors or need fewer implementation-heavy roles, where do your future seniors and managers come from?

**Prompts if the table stalls:**

- Do frontend/backend boundaries still hold?
- What happens to Scrum and coordination overhead?
- What becomes the manager's job if engineers ship faster?
- Which new role shows up on the org chart that did not exist in 2023

Participants mostly work in the same group at their home table, so to enable more personal interaction, this session breaks everyone out into 1:1 conversations with peers they have not spoken to yet. # Panel: The Future IT Organization


### **When**

17:00–18:00 (60 min). Dinner at 19:00.

### **Who**

- **Moderator:** Fabian, start to finish.
- **4 panelists:** picked live during the day, not pre-selected. Fabian listens at the participant round, war stories, lunch and breakouts, then announces the 4 at the 15:30 coffee break. Target mix: one listed or legacy voice, one hypergrowth scaleup, one small or services firm, one wildcard from whoever surprised the room during the day.
- **Silent note-taker:** one trainer captures 3 bullets per question

### **What**

By 17:00 the room has heard the frame (keynote), seen the tools (demo), the peer stories (war stories) and spent two hours in breakouts. The panel is the last plenary of the day before dinner and it should push one level up from adoption into org design: if implementation is no longer the bottleneck, what is the IT organization actually for?

This is not a conference "future of work" platitude panel. Fabian comes in with a sharp point of view and uses the panelists to stress-test it. Off the record. No recording. No slides. That is what unlocks honest answers.

- **Intro frame (5 min).** One sentence setting up the thesis. Introduce the 4 panelists by one line each, no CV, no company overview.
- **Moderated Q&A (45 min).** 5 or 6 pre-cooked questions, about 7 min each, with pre-cooked follow-ups for when panelists dodge into platitudes.
- **Room Q&A (10 min).** Hand-raised questions from the floor. Close with one line: "Dinner in 15 minutes. Keep arguing there."

### **Core questions (pick 5 or 6 on the day)**

1. If implementation is no longer the scarce resource, what is?
2. In two years, your engineering headcount is higher, the same, or lower. Pick one.
3. Name one role in your current org that will not exist in 2027.
4. Feature teams of 2 to 4 people as the core unit. True for your org, or wrong?
5. Painful migration vs. parallel reset. Which path are you actually on, and why didn't you pick the other one?
6. Can you lead this transformation if you are not hands-on with coding agents yourself?

### **Moderator job (Fabian)**

Don't hide behind neutral moderation. Interrupt politely but firmly, 90-second cap on first answers, loosen in round two. Names not titles. If a panelist gives a LinkedIn answer, use contrast: "Lukasz, you run 15 engineers. Andreas, you run thousands. Is this really the same problem?"
